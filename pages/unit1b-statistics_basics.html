Absolute & Relative – Solution

Absolute values describe an absolute frequency, total amount or a count. This means if you want to add up absolute values you calculate their sum. Examples for absolute values are the total area or total population of a country.
On the other hand, relative values describe a relative frequency or a value in relation to another. This means if you want to add up relative values you will need to calculate the weighted arithmetic mean. Examples for relative values are that South Africa is 2.1 times larger than Kenya or that 24.1% of people in Hessen live in poverty.
Relative values are easier to compare than absolute values but you need to always keep in mind how they were calculated.

Charts

Data can be visualized using charts. This way patterns, trends or clusters can be recognized and a deeper understanding of a dataset can be gained. There are different types of charts. The same data presented in another way can lead to other insights.

Unit 1 – Statistics

This page shares some tips and tricks for working with charts.

Charts – Solution

General tips to read a chart

  1. Take a look at the axes and ask yourself 'What do they show?', 'What kind of values do they show?', 'Where do the x and y values start?'.
  2. Think about the relation of the values. Relative values are easier to compare than absolute values.
  3. Do you notice maximum and minimum values, outlier values, clusters, patterns? Is the data spread?
  4. Where does the data come from? Who is responsible for the chart (e.g an expert or a company)? What are their motivations?

Charts in Maps – Solution

Recall the last section of the unit. The total population of a province, an absolute value, was displayed by a diagram with an area proportional to the province's population.
On the other hand, the population density, a relative value, was displayed through different shades of red and brown filling the province's boundaries.
This is the right method to display absolute and relative values in maps. Be advised not to take this for granted for every map as not all maps are made by professionals and therefore this is not always the case.

Reading a map is in some ways like reading a chart. When you are unsure, take a look at the legend, its scaling, and keep the general chart tips in mind.
