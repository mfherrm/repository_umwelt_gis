What are population pyramids?

Population pyramids are special histograms where values are stacked top to bottom from high to low. They usually illustrate the distribution of a country's or region's population.
Important information they convey is the population size which is shown on the x-axis and the corresponding age-groups which are represented on the y-axis.
Another characteristic is that they show both standardized as well as unstandardized values. This way, values are put into proportion and can be evaluated with little effort.

Take your time and try to understand what information you can obtain from a population pyramid. As soon as you're ready, move on to the first exercise by pressing the 'Next' button in the bottom right corner.

The term demographic transition describes a shift from high birth rates and high death rates to low birth rates and low death rates due to technological and societal advancement. Population pyramids can be used to better illustrate a country's stage in the demographic transition. There are four main stages which will be described in detail in the following.

1st Stage: In stage one there is a balance between high birth and death rates, thus resulting in little to no population growth. The population size is also constrained by the available food supply. This stage depicts pre-industrial society.
2nd Stage: In this stage, in which most developing countries are, death rates drop due to improvement of food supply and sanitation. Thus leading to an increase in life expectancy and a decrease in disease. This means that childhood mortality is also lower, leading to a broad pyramid base. After some time, this broad base leads to rapid population growth as the younger generations will start to have children of their own.
3rd Stage: Due to access to contraception, urbanization and an increase in education and status of women, birth rates fall. This ultimately results in stagnation of population.
4th Stage: In the fourth stage, both birth and death rates are low. This leads to an increase in average age and might even lead to an even further decrease in birth rates. Lower birth rates in turn mean that the death rate is higher than the birth rate resulting in a shrinking society.

Stages of demographic transition
  • 1st Stage - Slow growth
    • High death rate
    • High birth rate
    • Slow population growth
  • 2nd Stage - Rapid growth
    • Death rate falling rapidly
    • High birth rate
    • Rapid population growth
  • 3rd Stage - Stagnation
    • Low death rate
    • Declining birth rate
    • Slow population growth
  • 4th Stage - Contraction
    • Low death rate
    • Very low birth rate
    • Slow population decrease
